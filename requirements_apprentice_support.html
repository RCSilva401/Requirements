What is a requirement?

A statement of a feature, capability, or qualitative constraint that a product, process, or system must have to solve a business problem.

Defines the what, not the how
Clear and concise — no ambiguous filler words
Unambiguous — exactly one interpretation
Testable — binary pass/fail possible
Traceable — linked to ID, source, business goal

Requirements hierarchy

Business requirements — sponsor's view: strategic goals, compliance, revenue targets
User / stakeholder requirements — end-user view: tasks, inputs, outputs needed day-to-day
Functional system requirements — what the system must do: actions, behaviours, outputs
Non-functional system requirements — how well: performance, security, availability, usability

Common misconceptions to watch for

Misconception: "My job is to write down exactly what the stakeholder tells me."
Reality: Stakeholders state a messy mixture of symptoms, complaints, and premature solutions. Your job is to engineer what the business actually needs.
Misconception: "Requirements engineering is a linear, one-and-done process."
Reality: It is highly iterative — analysis reveals gaps that send you back to elicitation; validation errors loop back into analysis.
Misconception: "Non-functional requirements are optional."
Reality: Stakeholders assume NFRs are already met. Omitting them causes performance failures, security breaches, and accessibility failures at launch.

Block 1: Elicitation fundamentals

K12K13S12
The industry shifted from "Requirements Gathering" to "Requirements Elicitation" because requirements must be carefully drawn out — they are not already formed and waiting to be picked up. Stakeholders rarely know exactly what they need or can express it clearly.

Requirements vs solution descriptors K13

Solution descriptor — trap

"Deploy a high-end NLP conversational AI chatbot with database syncing."

Elicited requirement — goal

"Reduce customer service overhead and wait times during peak store enquiries."

Always ask "why" at least five times before accepting a stakeholder's stated solution. The Paris rugby match analogy: the requirement is being at the stadium for kick-off within budget — the specific train time is just one unvalidated option.
Distinction: Explicitly document your K13 mindset for your assessor — never assume they can infer it. Show you distinguished between eliciting requirements and gathering solution descriptions, with concrete project examples and contrasting outcomes.

The three classic elicitation barriers

Symptoms vs root causes

Stakeholders request fixes for surface symptoms. Use 5 Whys or Fishbone diagrams to dig deeper.

"Duck-rabbit" phenomenon

Different teams view the same process but perceive entirely different limitations. Resolve in a workshop.

Tacit knowledge

Experts execute complex tasks subconsciously and forget to mention crucial steps. Use observation to surface these.

Block 2: Documenting & QA

K14K16K17S13S14
Documentation is not an administrative task — it is a core analysis tool. The act of writing a requirement into a structured template forces you to find hidden logical gaps and structural flaws.

Approaches to categorising requirements K14S13

K14 asks you to explain the approaches. S13 asks you to demonstrate you applied them and to evaluate their advantages and disadvantages.

Business requirements
Written from the project sponsor's or executive's view. Define overall project scope and high-level objectives.
User / stakeholder requirements
Written from the end-user's view. Describe goals and inputs/outputs to complete daily operational tasks.
Functional system requirements
What the system must explicitly do — the distinct actions, workflows, and behaviours.
Non-functional system requirements
How well the system must execute — performance metrics, security limits, accessibility constraints.
Common fail: Mixing levels. End-users rarely care about corporate ROI — they care about their daily tasks. Keep these viewpoints separated in your documentation to ensure clear traceability.
Distinction — S13: To hit distinction, critically evaluate the advantages and disadvantages of your chosen documentation approach (e.g. agile user stories vs a formal requirements table) and explicitly explain when each is appropriate.
Local standards: Document requirements in strict alignment with your organisation's formatting rules, software standards (e.g. Jira user stories), or regional laws (e.g. GDPR, GDS guidelines). If no standard exists — create one and drive adoption.

Stakeholders rarely raise NFRs unprompted — they assume the system will be fast and secure. You must proactively prompt for these metrics in elicitation sessions, otherwise engineering teams build without them.
K16 justification principle: "Standard practice" is a weak justification. Instead, link each NFR to a specific strategic risk it mitigates (e.g. a £10m data compliance fine, basket abandonment revenue loss, reputational damage). This makes the development cost justifiable to project sponsors.

How easily users can complete tasks. Clear, logical interface layout. Drives adoption and reduces errors.

Ensuring all users — including those with disabilities — can access and use the system. WCAG 2.2 AA standard.

Block 3: Prioritisation & management

K14K15S15S16S17
Without prioritisation, teams treat all requests as equally important. When time runs short, developers build the easiest features rather than the most critical — resulting in an over-budget deployment that misses core business goals.

Requirements validation — S16 S16

Validation is not about getting your internal project team to agree. It is about obtaining formal, authorised sign-off from the stakeholders you elicited those requirements from.

Verification vs validation: Verification checks if requirements are written correctly (are they testable, clear?). Validation checks if they are the right requirements (do they actually solve the stakeholder's problem?).
TechniqueBest forAdvantageLimitation
Stakeholder walkthroughComplex requirements; non-technical stakeholders; high-stakes sign-offUncovers hidden gaps; drives consensusSignificant time commitment for all parties
Prototyping / MVPAgile sprints; visual confirmation; user journey validationMakes requirements tangible; great for non-technical audiencesRisk of scope fixation on visuals rather than data logic
InspectionFormal scrutiny; regulatory/compliance contextsRigorous; catches subtle ambiguitiesSlow; requires expert reviewers
Document sign-offSimple, well-understood requirements; time-constrained stakeholdersFast and low overhead"Passive email" risk — stakeholders may not read carefully
Distinction — reflection: For K14, explain how you would modify your validation approach in the future. If a document sign-off led to confusion during development, would you switch to a live walkthrough next time? Demonstrating this lessons-learned thinking is what separates pass from distinction.
The 4-step validation pathway: 1) Document → 2) Playback → 3) Refine → 4) Secure sign-off. Your S16 portfolio must show evidence of all four stages, not just the final signed document.

MoSCoW prioritisation framework S15

M — Must have
Critical path. System cannot function without this. Non-negotiable for the current phase.
S — Should have
Important but not vital for MVP. Defer if resources constrained.
C — Could have
Nice to have. Include only if time and budget permit after Musts and Shoulds are delivered.
W — Won't have
Explicitly excluded from this phase. Captured and deferred for future consideration.
"Everything is a Must" trap: Stakeholders naturally classify all requests as Must. Counter this by capping Must requirements at 60% of total project effort before the session begins — agree this rule upfront.
Common fail: Showing a MoSCoW column in the table without explaining the decision-making process and rules used to assign each level. Assessors want the methodology and story, not just the grid.
Include technical developers in prioritisation sessions so the team can accurately score effort and complexity in real time — preventing business stakeholders from prioritising technically infeasible features.
Distinction — S15: Compare at least 3 prioritisation methods (MoSCoW, RICE, cost-value analysis, weighted scoring) and justify why your chosen method fit the specific project context. Document who held sign-off authority and what objective rules defined a "Must" vs a "Should."

Requirements traceability K15S17

Horizontal traceability

Backwards: Trace a requirement back to its original stakeholder source or business goal. Vital for clarification and conflict resolution mid-project.

Forwards: Track a requirement through development, implementation, and testing to prove it has been resolved satisfactorily.

Vertical traceability

Traces a requirement up or down the hierarchy to ensure every detailed functional step still aligns with the overarching business strategy, objectives, and mission.

If a requirement changes: "Does this modification still help the business achieve what it originally set out to do?"

Gold-plating risk: If a developer adds an unrequested feature because it is technically easy, it fails vertical traceability — it cannot be mapped to any validated stakeholder or business objective, wasting development capital.

The 5-step change control workflow K15

1 — Gather information

Understand the exact purpose and need behind the change request. Is it genuinely worth the analytical effort?

2 — Determine scope

Is the change inside or outside the agreed project boundary? Going outside without control inflates budget and causes delays.

3 — Assess priority

Does this stem from a genuine operational need? Use predefined guidelines to navigate conflicting stakeholder opinions.

4 — Approve or reject

Route through your organisation's formal approval pathways. No informal verbal agreements — all changes need documented sign-off.

5 — Action

If approved, update all delivery artefacts: plans, schedules, business process documentation, and the central requirements matrix.

Agile myth: Working in Agile does not mean anyone can change anything without governance. Agile still requires changes to be evaluated against sprint velocity and business value before implementation.

Portfolio evidence — what to include

K15Requirements management including change control — knowledge
  • Describe approaches to requirements management, including change control processes
  • Explain the relevance and importance of managing requirements throughout a project
  • Describe how change control is conducted: capturing, monitoring, and approving changes
  • Explain the consequences of not adequately managing requirements
Blend your traceability matrix evidence (S17) directly with your change control story. Show how unique requirement IDs, documented owners, and version histories empower intelligent impact analysis when a stakeholder requests a late-stage change.
S15Prioritising requirements — skill
  • Demonstrate and justify prioritising requirements using an appropriate approach
  • Explain what factors influenced the process (time, cost, risk)
  • Explain the setting: who was involved, how it played out, what you learned
  • Show how prioritisation affected the overall project outcome
  • Describe how you resolved conflicts or disagreements during prioritisation
Distinction: Don't just show a MoSCoW table. Show the methodology — what rules were set beforehand, who held sign-off authority, how you handled "everything is a Must", and how you would use objective data weighting (RICE) to counter that pressure.
S16Validating requirements — skill
  • Provide evidence that you validated requirements with your actual stakeholders (not just your project team)
  • Show the technique you used and justify why you chose it over alternatives
  • Provide proof: sign-off emails, meeting screenshots, annotated wireframes, walkthrough notes
  • Reflect on what worked well and what you would do differently next time
Validation is not about getting your internal team to agree with you. It is about formal sign-off from the stakeholders you originally elicited requirements from. A "looks good" reply to a 50-page email is not adequate validation evidence.
S17Requirements traceability — skill
  • Evidence the establishment of requirements traceability in your project documentation
  • Annotate or explain your traceability matrix: what it shows, why it was beneficial
  • Provide an artefact or screenshot of your RTM and talk through the key columns
  • Use the terms horizontal and vertical traceability to elevate your portfolio language
Note: you do not have to manage every requirement across an entire multi-year project to satisfy S17. You need to demonstrate that you supported the establishment of a tracing system — showing how you set it up and why each column matters is sufficient.

Activity 1 · Bloom's: Understand · K12
Why did the industry shift its terminology from "Requirements Gathering" to "Requirements Elicitation"?
A. Because "gathering" is too informal a term for professional practice
B. Because technology has made gathering data automated, leaving only elicitation to humans
C. Because requirements are rarely fully formed — they must be carefully drawn out through active investigation
D. Because elicitation is a legal requirement under UK standards
Correct. "Gathering" incorrectly implies requirements are already waiting to be picked up. In reality, stakeholders rarely know exactly what they need or can express it clearly. Elicitation recognises that requirements must be drawn out through active analysis, questioning, and professional investigation.
Not quite. The key issue is that requirements are not pre-formed and waiting to be collected. Stakeholders rarely articulate their true needs clearly — they must be actively elicited through professional investigation techniques.
Activity 2 · Bloom's: Apply · K13
A stakeholder says: "We need to hire three extra service staff because our checkout queues are too slow." What should you do first as a business analyst?
A. Document the requirement: "Hire 3 additional service staff to reduce checkout time"
B. Escalate to the project sponsor to approve the hiring budget
C. Ask why the queues are slow — investigate the root cause before accepting this solution descriptor
D. Note this as a business requirement and move to documenting functional requirements
Correct. The stakeholder has given a solution descriptor ("hire staff"), not a requirement. The root cause may be slow point-of-sale software. The elicited requirement might be: "The order logging process must consistently process and track customer purchases in under 30 seconds." This opens up far better, cheaper solutions than simply adding headcount.
This falls into the solution descriptor trap. The stakeholder has proposed a solution before the root cause has been investigated. Your job is to uncover why queues are slow — it might be a software issue, not a staffing one.
Activity 3 · Bloom's: Analyze · K12 & S12
A BA needs to map an undocumented legacy system used by a highly time-constrained operations team. Which combination of techniques would be most effective?
A. Large-scale survey + focus group + prototyping
B. Document analysis + observation/shadowing + targeted 1-on-1 interview with a system super-user
C. Three rounds of workshops with all stakeholders simultaneously
D. Prototyping + survey + document sign-off
Correct. Document analysis establishes the baseline without interrupting staff. Shadowing during peak hours catches actual workarounds. A targeted 1-on-1 interview with a super-user clarifies specific system rules. This combination maximises discovery depth while respecting tight stakeholder availability.
Consider the constraints — the team is highly time-constrained and the system is undocumented. You need techniques that minimise stakeholder disruption while maximising depth of discovery. Which combination achieves both?
Activity 4 · Bloom's: Apply · K14 & S13 (written)
Rewrite this poor requirement so it is clear, unambiguous, and testable:

"The platform must be highly reliable."
Think about: what metric makes reliability testable? What time period applies? What recovery time is acceptable?
"The system must maintain 99.95% operational availability during core business hours (08:00–18:00, Monday to Friday), with automated recovery protocols completing within 15 minutes of an outage."

Why this works: Percentage availability is measurable, the time period is specified, and recovery time is quantified — a QA engineer can now design a binary pass/fail test case.
Activity 5 · Bloom's: Analyze · K16
Classify this requirement and justify which NFR category it belongs to:

"The portal must restrict access to sensitive citizen case histories using Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), verifying identity via a secure centralised gateway."
A. Functional system requirement — it describes what the system must do
B. Non-functional system requirement (security) — it defines an operational constraint around how well data is protected
C. User requirement — it describes what the end user needs
D. Business requirement — it relates to a regulatory business goal
Correct. This is a non-functional system requirement (security category). It defines an operational constraint — how well data is protected — rather than a direct interactive user feature or action. The justification is risk mitigation and statutory compliance, protecting against unauthorised access under national privacy legislation.
Not quite. While it has elements of what the system must do, the primary focus is on the quality property of security — how well the system protects data. This makes it a non-functional requirement in the security category.
Activity 6 · Bloom's: Analyze · S14 (written)
You receive two requirements from different stakeholders:

Source A: "The sales dashboard must update performance figures every 5 seconds."
Source B: "The database must only sync records overnight at 2 AM to minimise server strain."

How would you apply S14 to resolve this conflict?
Flag this as a critical structural conflict during the analysis phase. The underlying data needs differ: Source A requires timely data visibility; Source B requires operational server stability. Bring both parties together to negotiate a middle-ground — such as a decoupled caching layer or hourly mini-batch processing — and convert the conflicting entries into a single, cohesive, technically feasible requirement. Document the original conflicting versions, the resolution process, and the final agreed requirement as your S14 evidence.
Activity 7 · Bloom's: Evaluate · S16
A project team proposes skipping formal validation and moving straight from analysis to development, arguing that "stakeholders already told us what they wanted during elicitation." Evaluate the risk.
A. Low risk — elicitation captured everything needed
B. Medium risk — some gaps may emerge but can be fixed during testing
C. High risk — analysis may have introduced misinterpretations that only validation can catch
D. No risk if the elicitation was well documented
Correct. This is high risk. Elicitation captures raw, often conflicting desires. Analysis cleans and structures those inputs into technical specifications, which may introduce misinterpretations. Skipping validation means you miss the chance to confirm the restructured requirements still match actual stakeholder needs. You may deliver a system that works technically but fails operationally.
The key risk is that analysis can introduce misinterpretations. Raw elicitation outputs are messy and conflicting — after analysis structures them, stakeholders must confirm the structured version still matches what they originally needed.
Activity 8 · Bloom's: Evaluate · S13 distinction
An analyst creates an excellent new requirements template for their project but does not archive the process or share it with the team. Does this meet the distinction standard for S13?
A. Yes — creating a high-quality template is sufficient for distinction
B. No — distinction requires evidence of contributing to local standards across the wider organisation, not just one individual project
C. Yes — the template itself is the evidence assessors look for
D. Partially — it meets distinction if the template is very detailed
Correct. Creating a template satisfies individual compliance (pass level), but completely fails the distinction standard. Distinction requires active evidence of contributing to the enhancement, maintenance, or adoption of local standards across the wider organisation. Without archiving, coaching peers, or running training to drive adoption, the artefact remains isolated and cannot be repeated for future projects.
Think about what "contributing to local standards" means organisationally. A template used by one person on one project has no lasting impact on the organisation. Distinction requires evidence that your work elevated standards for others.

Discussion question answers

Q: Why do most software defects originate in requirements rather than coding?
Developers build exactly what is documented. If the document contains logical flaws, missing rules, ambiguities, or wrong assumptions, the developer constructs a technically flawless implementation of the wrong feature. A perfect build of the wrong requirement is still a failure.
Q: Why dedicate separate modules to Documenting vs Prioritisation & Management?
Documenting establishes the exact baseline capabilities and performance metrics of the required state. Prioritisation and Management protect that baseline against scope creep by controlling how requirements shift under real-world delivery constraints. Both are essential but serve fundamentally different governance purposes.
Q: Can a system have high usability but poor UX?
Yes. A corporate expenses form may be efficient (high usability — zero errors, fast task completion) but leave the user feeling cold and frustrated (poor UX). Usability is about effectiveness and efficiency; UX covers all subjective feelings and perceptions about the entire interaction.
Q: Why is "risk mitigation" a stronger NFR justification than "it's standard practice"?
"Standard practice" is subjective and difficult to fund. Proving that a security requirement mitigates a specific quantifiable risk — such as a £10m data compliance fine or a catastrophic database breach — ties the requirement directly to financial and regulatory protection, making the development cost justifiable to project sponsors.
